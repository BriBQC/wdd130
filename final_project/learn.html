Asanas

What are Asanas?

Asana is sanskrit for "posture". Yoga poses are asanas. These are poses that make up the flows we practice in class. Asanas are the third limb of ashtanga yoga and the what most people associate yoga with. Our classes offer a variety of asanas from beginner to advanced levels. Instructors in our classes will demonstrate the basic pose and offer variations based off the needs of the students. Every pose has a variation for every body type and learning level.

Examples:

Urdhva Mukha Shvanasana

Upward Facing Dog

Dhanurasana

Bow Pose

Astavakrasana

Eight Angle Pose

Chakras

What are Chakras?

The sanskrit word chakra means "wheel". Chakras are wheels of energy located along the central spine. These wheels manage energy for areas of the body and connect with each other. These wheels receive energy, turn it into the form you need, then transmit it along the energy lines of your body. The also do this when it comes to energy that needs to leave the body. There are seven chakras as shown in the image below. 1. Sahsrara or crown chakra. 2. Ajna or third-eye chakra. 3. Vissudha or throat chakra. 4. Anahata or heart chakra. 5. Manipura or solar plexus chakra. 6. Svadhisthana or sacral chakra. And 7. Muladhara or root chakra. Each one is unique in its location, element, goals, and connection. Intentions

What are Intentions?

We set intentions at the beginning of every class. Intentions set the mood for your practice and help us bring what we gain during that pracitice along with us as we re-enter our regular day. Intentions can be anything you want them to be. Our instructors will encourage you to set your intentions to something you feel you need to work or focus on in your life, or something that you need to see or feel more at this time, or even to something that you want to invite into your life at this time. Intentions can be difficult for some because they are so broad in scope. Check out the examples below to get you started.

Examples:

  • Gratitude
  • Forgiveness
  • Healing
  • Patience
  • Connection
  • Feeling
  • Open
  • Kindness
  • Love

Mantras

What are Mantras?

A mantra is a "tool for the mind" and a mantra practice is also called the yoga of sound. Most often used to awaken the chakras and help energy to flow. Mantras are sounds, vibrations that awaken our consciousness. Mantras can be audible or inward, spoken in our minds. Audible mantras are good for connecting within a group and inward mantras are good for personal connection deep within. Mantras can be traditional like the following examples or they can be words that have speical meaning to us.

Om:

This is the base sound that carries all other sounds with it through the universe. This mantra will bring you into balance with the world around you which in turn brings insights, connections, and renewed energy.

So Hum:

This mantra means "I Am". Heard in most yoga classes, it is a wonderful mantra to use as you breathe to remind you of your self and how you are connected to this moment, this world, this universe. A centering and calmind mantra to focus on breath and life.

Sham:

This mantra is perfect to use if you need to let go of anything that no longer serves you. Great for addictions, stress, anxiety, hoarding, or any other type of excessiveness that you need to practice letting go. Sham will focus your mind and help you find a new source of power within.

Or create your own like these:

  • I am
  • I am love
  • I am enough
  • Breathe in breathe out
  • Imagine the possibilities
  • Awaken the fire within
  • Be at peace
  • Be the change
  • Be exceptional

Mudras

What are Mudras?

Mudra in sanskrit means "seal". Most are used to "seal" and direct the flow of energy through the body. Hand or "hasta" mudras are the most common and are helpful to create balance, cleanse, and heal the body. Based upon placement of the fingers and hands different types of energy can be directed to specific parts of the body. Here at Breathe we use hasta mudras in all our classes. here are the main ones you will see being used at our studio:

Anjali

Or prayer position as you'll hear it most often referred, Anjali has multiple uses around the world including: pranayama, meditation, greeting, and reverence. Anjali mudra is used to connect mind, body, and heart. The hands coming together represent mind connecting with body and the two hands touching the heart brings all three into balance.

Dhyana

Best used during meditation practice, Dhyana mudra is about balance, calmness, concentration, and healing. The left palm, representing illusion, supports the right palm, representing awareness, with the thumbs resting gently together. This pose is easier to hold for longer periods of time in meditation or sitting pranayama practices than some other mudras.

Gyan

Just about everyone has seen this mudra. It is commonly associated with yoga in all forms of media. Because of its familiarity and ease to form, it is a great mudra to start trying as a beginner. This mudra connects our will with that of the universe or your higher power. Gyan helps us connect to deeper understanding.

Kali

This mudra embodies the fire, power, and strength of the Hindu Goddess Kali. Used often during yoga practice to aid in tension release, bring feelings of strength and fire, and banish negativity. Try standing tall with your arms overhead in Kali mudra and not feel powerful!

Prana

Awaken your energy with prana mudra! This powerful mudra helps guide our Life force energy within our body. Used as part of a pranayama or meditation practice, prana mudra can help with feelings of sluggishness or lack of motivation. We have some specially designed energy flow pranayama practices in our online video library.

Vishnu

Used primarily in the pranayama practice of Nadi Shodhana to help clear the nasal passages and energy centers of the brain. You can alternate between blocking the left and right nostrils with this mudra. Vishnu also balances the three base chakras so energy can move higher along the spine.

Sanskrit

What is Sanskrit?

Sanskrit is an ancient Indian language. Sanskrit words are deep in meaning - Sanskrit itself means language of the gods. This language is used most yoga often in yoga classes when teachers are referring to poses as all yoga poses have a sanskrit version of their name. Our classes also incorporate several other sanskrit words. You'll hear our teachers always offering the english translation of any sanskrit used in class. Learning a little sanskrit can be helpful to get started with any yoga class or broaden your experience further if you practice regularly. Here are a few words you will hear often in our classes:

Dhyana

Meditation

The seventh limb of ashtanga yoga involving effortless concentration and freedom from mind chatter.

Drishti

Vision

Where we focus our view during the day. During practice a focal point to aid in balance.

Namaste

Greeting

Meaning "I bow to you". The divine in us honors the divine in others.

Prana

Life force

The breath is our life force. As we focus on breathing we can find peace.

Satya

Truth

The practice of living truthfully with ourselves and others. Being honest.

Tapas

Fire

The heat generated when you practice. The fire inside that drives us.

Yoga

Unite

Practicing yoga brings a union between self and a higher power. Yoga creates connections
